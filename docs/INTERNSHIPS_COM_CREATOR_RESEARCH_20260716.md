## Cross-source patterns

1. Name the student problem in the first clause. Questions, explicit answers,
   warnings, and loss-avoidance frames recur in the top observed search cohort.
2. Make the advice concrete. Projects, coursework, coffee-chat questions,
   events, deadlines, and application checks are more specific than generic
   motivation.
3. Use a repeatable container. “Day N,” “Part N,” a short checklist, an
   opportunity alert, or an intern-life series makes the next episode legible.
4. Pair utility with identity. Niche creators anchor advice in a recognizable
   student path such as computer science, cybersecurity, engineering, finance,
   or international relations.
5. Human stakes make instructional content less sterile. Rejection humor,
   anxiety, intern etiquette, and day-in-the-life framing appear alongside
   tactical advice.
6. Outcome-heavy claims are common but unsafe to inherit. Interview counts,
   offer counts, referral multipliers, and guaranteed win rates require direct
   evidence and should not become Internships.com claims.

Metadata markers such as `#greenscreen`, `DIML`, `vlog`, reply framing, and a
pause-to-read instruction indicate possible format families. Because competitor
media was not downloaded or visually analyzed, this report does not claim exact
shot order, pacing, typography, or editing style.

## Internships.com adaptation

The first generated-video concept is **Close the proof gap**:

- Hook: “No experience? Don’t invent it—close the proof gap.”
- Visual metaphor: a role requirement falls through a gap in a generic
  application, then truthful coursework, project, and club evidence closes it.
- Sequence: problem → earned proof → focused tailoring → student review.
- CTA position: after the useful action, not before it.
- Promise boundary: no internship, referral, interview, response, or offer is
  guaranteed.

This combines problem-first phrasing, a concrete checklist, and a repeatable
visual system without copying any source title, script, footage, shot sequence,
identity, or claim.
