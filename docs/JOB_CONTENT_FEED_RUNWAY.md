## Content grammar

Runway's founder-led content most often uses:

- a firm reframe: the assumed answer is wrong or incomplete;
- a high-stakes job-search moment such as an interview, offer, resignation, or
  networking request;
- explicit `what not to say` versus `what to say instead`;
- numbered steps or a reusable response script;
- a short concluding rule that compresses the lesson;
- save, question, comment-keyword, guide, or link-in-bio CTAs after the lesson.

The bounded topic mix included:

- 5 general career posts;
- 4 interview posts;
- 3 networking posts;
- 1 broader job-search strategy post.

Nine of the 13 unique captions were long explainers. Five used a warning or
contrarian hook, and six began as direct statements.

## What to learn

The strongest transferable principle is **specific authority**:

1. name the decision or fear;
2. reject the vague default advice;
3. give the exact process, checklist, or language;
4. finish with one memorable operating rule;
5. introduce the product only after the advice has value on its own.

Internships.com can adapt this into truthful student-facing formats such as:

- “Don’t copy the job description. Translate it into proof you earned.”
- “A referral message is a research question, not an ask for a job.”
- “No internship yet? Here are three forms of earned proof you can build this
  month.”

Those examples are original adaptations, not copied Runway scripts.
