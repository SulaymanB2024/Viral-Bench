# Job-Search Content Language — 2026-07-16

## Research question

What does effective-looking short-form content in the job-search and internship
realm sound like, and how should Internships.com develop an original version of
that language?

This synthesis uses 65 unique public TikTok metadata records:

- 13 from Runway founder Ford Coleman;
- 16 from Handshake's official profile;
- 36 from a reviewed internship-search category proxy.

The sample is bounded and time-stamped. Popular-post metrics are observations,
not causal proof.

## The category's emotional vocabulary

Job-search content repeatedly works around six audience states:

1. **Late** — the role opened already; other people are applying.
2. **Unqualified** — the student thinks they lack experience or proof.
3. **Overwhelmed** — too many boards, applications, deadlines, and steps.
4. **Awkward** — networking, emails, interviews, and asking for help feel
   socially risky.
5. **Invisible** — the student is applying but getting no response.
6. **Uncertain** — they do not know which role, résumé change, or next action is
   worth their time.

Strong content does not stay in the emotion. It converts the emotion into one
specific next action.

## The three dominant voices

### 1. The operator

“Here is the process. Here are the words. Here is what to do next.”

This voice appears most strongly in the Runway founder sample. It is
authoritative, structured, and comfortable challenging generic career advice.

Useful structure:

`hard reframe → exact steps or script → memorable rule → optional CTA`

### 2. The peer

“You know this awkward or exhausting feeling? Me too.”

This voice appears most strongly in Handshake's short and relatable posts. It
uses identity, major, internship life, reactions, and low-friction questions.

Useful structure:

`recognizable moment → social reaction → one useful insight`

### 3. The radar

“This opened. Here is who it is for. Here is the deadline.”

This voice dominates the opportunity-alert portion of the category sample.

Useful structure:

`program + year → fit → pay/location → deadline → verified application path`

## Repeated hook families

- “Do not make this mistake.”
- “Here is what to say instead.”
- “These roles or applications just opened.”
- “Three ways to…”
- “How I got…”
- “Calling all [major or student type].”
- “Nobody tells students this.”
- “Before you send another application…”

Internships.com should use the underlying intent, not copy the expression.

## What Internships.com should sound like

The original house voice should be:

- direct without pretending certainty;
- useful before promotional;
- student-respectful rather than scolding;
- specific about evidence, dates, and requirements;
- lightly irreverent when describing broken job-search rituals;
- visibly human-reviewed at the point of application.

Recommended formula:

`student problem → one verified signal → one truthful action → uncertainty boundary → product role`

Example:

> You do not need to invent experience. Pick one requirement, find the closest
> real project or responsibility, and write the proof in the employer's
> language. Internships.com can draft the match; you decide whether it is true.

## First five repeatable content series

1. **Opportunity radar**
   - current role or program;
   - first-party timestamp;
   - who appears eligible;
   - deadline and source;
   - no guarantee of availability or selection.
2. **Application leak check**
   - one generic sentence;
   - why it loses specificity;
   - one truthful replacement.
3. **Close the proof gap**
   - one requirement;
   - one course, project, club, or work example;
   - one accurate evidence statement.
4. **Coffee chat without the cringe**
   - one low-pressure research question;
   - no referral entitlement;
   - one follow-up rule.
5. **Internship reality check**
   - a common overconfident claim;
   - what is actually knowable;
   - the next controllable step.

## Testing sequence

For the first owned Internships.com posts, test three distinct content modes
instead of blending everything into one format:

1. operator: detailed application fix;
2. peer: compact student-recognition post;
3. radar: verified opportunity alert.

Track hook retention, completion, saves, shares, comments, profile visits, and
CTA actions. After at least three posts per mode, compare owned performance
before declaring a house-format winner.

## Safety and originality

- Never copy a collected script, title, shot sequence, visual identity, or
  creator likeness.
- Never publish an opportunity without rechecking its official source and date.
- Never promise an internship, referral, interview, response, or offer.
- Keep the student responsible for reviewing and sending any application.
