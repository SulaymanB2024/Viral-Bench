# Handshake Content Feed Notes

Content source: TikTok `@joinhandshake`

Relationship: official brand profile. Handshake's public site links to this
exact TikTok account.

## Bounded sample

- 16 unique profile posts;
- 8 recent-cohort posts;
- 8 popular-cohort posts;
- no downloaded media.

The observed median view count was `21,994.5`. Recent posts in this small sample
were much newer and generally had less accumulated distribution than the
retrospective popular cohort, so the number is descriptive only.

## Content grammar

Handshake uses a lighter and more socially native mix than the Runway founder
source:

- short captions and compact talking points;
- student-life or job-search reactions;
- major-specific identity callouts;
- pause-to-read education;
- questions about internships and workplace experience;
- occasional longer career guidance.

The bounded format mix included:

- 9 short talking points;
- 5 meme or reaction posts;
- 1 list explainer;
- 1 long explainer.

Thirteen of 16 posts had no explicit CTA. Five hooks were classified as
relatable identity, six as direct statements, and three as questions.

## What to learn

The transferable principle is **make utility feel socially recognizable**.
Career advice does not always need to arrive as a lecture. It can begin with:

- the awkward email everyone overthinks;
- the feeling that job searching is itself a full-time job;
- a major-specific callout;
- a question about an office perk or internship moment;
- a short reaction followed by one useful next step.

For Internships.com, this suggests a deliberate relief valve between heavier
instructional posts:

1. one high-utility application or opportunity post;
2. one student-recognition or intern-life post;
3. one product-proof post;
4. repeat with a new major, role family, or search problem.

## Boundaries

- Do not recreate Handshake's memes, campaign concepts, copy, or visual system.
- Brand relatability must not become humiliation of students or job seekers.
- A Handshake post is content evidence, not proof that an opening remains
  available.
